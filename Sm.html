Samarium (Sm)

Basic Information

Atomic Number: 62

Atomic Mass: 150.36

Symbol: Sm

Atomic Structure

  • Protons: 62
  • Neutrons: Varies by isotope (commonly ~88)
  • Electrons: 62

Electron Configuration

[Xe] 4f6 6s2

Isotopes

  • Sm-144 (stable)
  • Sm-147 (used in dating rocks and meteorites)
  • Sm-152 (used in radiotherapy)

Physical Properties

  • State: Solid
  • Melting Point: 1072 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1900 °C
  • Density: 7.52 g/cm³

Chemical Properties

  • Reacts slowly with oxygen and water
  • Forms trivalent compounds (Sm³⁺)

Occurrence

Found in minerals such as monazite and bastnäsite, often extracted during the processing of rare earth elements.

Applications

  • Samarium–cobalt magnets (high-performance permanent magnets)
  • Used in nuclear reactors to absorb neutrons
  • Samarium-153 used in treatment of bone cancer pain

Historical Background

Discovered in 1879 by Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran, named after the mineral samarskite, itself named after Russian engineer Vasili Samarsky-Bykhovets.

Safety & Toxicity

Low toxicity in small amounts but should be handled with care in powdered or radioactive form. Environmental buildup can be problematic near mining sites.

Position in Periodic Table

Group: Lanthanides, Period: 6, f-block

Interesting Facts

Despite being a rare earth element, samarium is more abundant in the Earth's crust than tin.
