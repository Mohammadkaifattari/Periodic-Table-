Helium (He)

Basic Information

Atomic Number: 2

Atomic Mass: 4.0026

Symbol: He

Atomic Structure

  • Protons: 2
  • Neutrons: Usually 2 (in He-4 isotope)
  • Electrons: 2

Electron Configuration

1s2

Isotopes

  • He-3: Stable, rare isotope
  • He-4: Most common, stable isotope

Physical Properties

  • State: Gas (at room temperature)
  • Melting Point: −272.2 °C (under high pressure)
  • Boiling Point: −268.93 °C
  • Density: 0.1786 g/L at 0 °C

Chemical Properties

  • Inert (non-reactive)
  • Belongs to the noble gases group
  • Forms almost no compounds

Occurrence

Helium is found in natural gas deposits and also produced during radioactive decay of heavy elements underground. It is the second most abundant element in the universe.

Applications

  • Used in balloons and airships (non-flammable)
  • Cooling for MRI machines and superconductors
  • Used in gas chromatography and space industry

Historical Background

Discovered in 1868 by Jules Janssen and Norman Lockyer while observing the sun. It was named after the Greek word "Helios" meaning "sun". First isolated on Earth by Sir William Ramsay in 1895.

Safety & Toxicity

Helium is non-toxic and inert, but inhaling it in place of oxygen can cause suffocation. Inhaling helium from balloons can cause dizziness or unconsciousness.

Interesting Facts

  • Second lightest and second most abundant element in the universe
  • Does not solidify at atmospheric pressure, even near absolute zero
  • Used in deep-sea breathing gas mixtures
